Will Calisthenics Make You a Stronger Weightlifter?

By Owen Ezell
June 19, 2024

This was the question on my mind one day a few weeks ago as I loaded a hex bar with 315 lbs of plates. It was my first time deadlifting in five years, and in that time, the only strength training I had done was strictly bodyweight: push-ups, pull-ups, bodyweight squats, planks, hip bridges, etc.

Now it was time to see if all that calisthenics work would translate into deadlift strength. I stepped into the bar, grabbed the handles, set my knees and back, and pulled. 315 lbs came up pretty easily. I locked out at the top, then set the weight down again under control.

I was, and still am, pretty ecstatic about this result. After 5 years of nothing but calisthenics, I’ve maintained my ability to pull a heavy weight off the floor. My deadlift max as a beginner weightlifter, before switching to calisthenics, was also 315. Granted, that was with a straight bar and this was with a hex bar (which is easier), but still, 315 is a decent pull for a non-lifter no matter which bar you use.

Also, the lift in the video was after a two-mile run. I’m confident that if I tried again with fresh legs, I could hit 335 lbs, possibly even more.

Calisthenics Can Replace the Deadlift After All

My main takeaway from this experience is that the lower-body calisthenics moves that I have been practicing for years are a valid substitute for deadlifts. So I can continue to focus on unweighted pistol squats, hip bridges, etc. without worrying about losing my ability to deadlift heavy.

What’s interesting about this is that calisthenics is often supposed to be inadequate for the lower body, especially when it comes to hip-hinge movements. The common idea is that while calisthenics is great for building upper-body strength, you need to use external weights to build up the big muscles of your legs. This notion circulates even among calisthenics advocates. The “Recommended Routine” on r/bodyweightfitness calls for integrating barbell squats and deadlifts into your workouts once you can manage one-leg bodyweight squat variations.

But my little experiment calls this notion into question. Apparently, there is a large degree of carryover between standard calisthenics moves and the deadlift, at least for me. And if that’s the case, why should I add deadlifts to my calisthenics routine?

Use Calisthenics to Unlock “Free Gains” on the Deadlift

Philip Chubb articulates a similar point on his excellent YouTube and Instagram account, Mindful Mover. Chubb calls it “free gains” — the idea is that by working on certain exercises, you can make gains in other exercises for “free”, that is without working those exercises directly.

Evidently, calisthenics has allowed me to make “free gains” in the deadlift, and Chubb himself has documented the same thing. He tested his deadlift strength after three years of calisthenics-based lower-body training and was able to pull 315 lbs on a straight bar.

Matt Schifferle, another calisthenics-only athlete, performed a similar deadlift test with the same positive result.

Apparently, you don’t need to deadlift to be able to deadlift.

Does Calisthenics Translate to Overhead Press Strength?

Three days after my triumphant deadlift test, I was back in the gym looking to see what other “free gains” I had made from calisthenics. This time, I wanted to try the overhead press, another lift I hadn’t attempted for years. Would my push-up and handstand work carry over as extra pressing power?

Overhead press was always my weak link as a lifter. I was never able to manage more than 135 lbs. Still, I felt confident that I would be able to hit at least this number after 5 years of calisthenics training. In that time, I had added some visible muscle to my arms and torso, muscle which I thought would help me literally raise the bar.

So what happened? I totally failed!

Before trying 135 lbs, I figured I would warm up with lighter weight. I did an empty bar (45 lbs) then 95 lbs without issue. Then I added 10 lbs to either side for a total of 115 lbs and tried that. I managed one rep but couldn’t get the bar past my head on the second rep.

I racked the weight, paused for a minute to muster my strength, then tried again. Same result — I couldn’t get 115 lbs past my chin.

There was no point in trying 135 lbs if I couldn’t lift a lighter weight. The test had failed.

Why Calisthenics Improved My Deadlift But Not Overhead Press

I was dismayed by this result, but also a little intrigued. There is a plethora of great calisthenics exercises for the upper-body — push-ups, pull-ups, dips, rows, handstands — and many of these are highly regarded even by iron-worshippers. Nobody doubts that you can build a strong upper-body with bodyweight exercises — just look at gymnasts! Yet, in my case, years of calisthenics failed to produce gains in one of the most basic markers of upper-body strength, the overhead press. Meanwhile, the one lift which everyone assumes has no calisthenics equivalent, the deadlift, is as strong as ever after years of calisthenics-only training. What gives?

My current explanation is that calisthenics produces weightlifting gains only when it mimics weightlifting with regard to movement and range of motion in the joints.

Take the deadlift, which involves straightening of the ankle, knee, and hip joints. My observation is that it is actually pretty easy to mimic this motion using calisthenics. Both two-leg and one-leg squat variations involve the exact same movement at the exact same joints. So it’s no wonder that bodyweight squat work seems to carry over as deadlift strength.

The overhead press, on the other hand, is a little bit different. Its bodyweight equivalents are the pike push-up and handstand push-up. These exercises involve the same movement at the same joints: flexion of the shoulder and extension of the elbow. However, their range of motion is different. During an overhead press, the weight must travel from the level to your shoulders past your head until your arms are locked out. During a pike push-up or handstand push-up, your head’s in the way, meaning that the weight only needs to travel from the top of your head to the lock out position.

Put simply, the overhead press has a bigger range of motion than pike push-ups and handstand push-ups. This, I think, is why the latter exercises don’t translate to the former. The hardest part of an overhead press is the bottom part of the motion, i.e. getting the weight off your shoulders to the level of your eyes. Crucially, this is the exact part of the motion that I wasn’t training with calisthenics.

Follow-Up n=1 Exercise Experiments

If my theory about calisthenics-to-weightlifting carryover is correct, then I should be able to improve my overhead press if I increase the range of motion of my pike push-ups. (It’s pretty easy to do this using gymnastics rings or a suspension trainer.) To test this out, all I have to do is practice increased-ROM pike push-ups for a while then head back to the gym to retest my overhead press 1-rep max. I’ll report back once I do.

My theory also implies that I should be able to improve, not just maintain, my deadlift with focused work in bodyweight squats, hip bridges, and other exercises involving the same joints and ROM. I’ll plan to run this test later. For now, I want to focus my leg training on running. More specifically, I want to see whether sprinting improves my endurance performance, measured by the time it takes to run two miles. My hypothesis is that it will because again — same joint movement, same range of motion. I’ll report back on this too.

Here’s to testing things out for yourself.
