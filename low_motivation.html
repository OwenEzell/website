The Cure for Low Workout Motivation

Low motivation is a sign from your psychology that your workout routine is literally not worth it — that its costs are higher than its benefits.

By Owen Ezell
August 8, 2023

If you haven’t heard of him, you should check out Matt Schifferle of the Red Delta Project. He has some wonderfully contrarian and, more importantly, helpful ideas about exercise and working out.

One of his ideas that interests me the most is his theory of human motivation. According to him, we feel motivated to take action in proportion to how high the benefits of that action are compared to the costs. So basically, motivation is a function of the cost-benefit ratio of the behavior in question.

I’m not sure if this is actually how motivation works on a psychological level, but I think it’s a helpful framework for making sense of our behavior. We’re much more likely to, say, eat an ice-cream sandwich if we have one in the freezer than if we have to drive to the store to get one. Why? Because the cost of walking to the freezer to get the sandwich is much lower than the cost of driving to the store to buy one.

Of course, what really drives our behavior is the perceived cost-to-benefit ratio, especially the short-term perceived cost-to-benefit ratio. If you have diabetes and the ice-cream sandwich happens to be really bad for you (i.e. has a high actual cost), this won’t be reflected in your behavior if you don’t know you have diabetes. Or maybe you are aware that you have diabetes but eat the ice-cream sandwich anyway because the benefits (the taste of the ice-cream) manifest immediately while the costs (worsened health) manifest later.

In any case, the cost-to-benefit framework shows us what’s going wrong when we’re struggling to maintain a workout habit: the costs are too high compared to the benefits. Or at least that’s what our perception is. This implies that the way to regain motivation is to find a way to cut the perceived costs of exercise while maintaining or increasing the perceived benefits. So how can we do this?

Cut the Cost of Exercise by Ditching the Gym

One of the best ways to reduce the perceived cost of working out is to ditch the gym. Gym memberships cost money. Getting there takes time, energy, and, if you have to take a car, money. And being there also takes time, in the form of hanging around while you wait for the dude who’s been on his phone for the past 15 minutes to finally relinquish the chest press.

Another hidden cost of going to the gym is the mental energy it takes to work out in front of other people. For introverts like me, this is a non-negligible factor. I am so much more comfortable working out alone or with someone I know.

What’s interesting is that these costs have nothing to do with the act of working out itself. Instead, they stem solely from the location where it is assumed exercise must take place, namely the gym. The good news is that exercise doesn’t need to take place at the gym, which means these costs can safely be eliminated without compromising your workout and the benefits it brings.

You may be thinking: “How do I work out without the gym?” Well, there are countless ways. You could go for a run or a bike ride, do some pull-ups and push-ups at the local playground, or do some sprints on a nearby field. All of these are financially and psychologically cheaper than going to the gym.

I should acknowledge here that not everyone has the same experience of the gym. I have friends who insist that they love the gym and wouldn’t work out anywhere else. For them, a gym membership only brings benefits: it’s a way to get out of the house, meet other people, etc. But I’d wager that for most of us, the gym is a serious drain on our workout motivation. In which case, why not work out elsewhere?

Increase the Benefits of Exercise by Upping the Intensity

So that’s one way to reduce the psychological costs of a workout habit. But what about the other side of the motivation equation — increasing the perceived benefits?

Here again, I think the answer is surprisingly simple: one of the best ways to increase the perceived benefits of your workout is to up the intensity of what you’re doing. What if you picked a couple of safe, basic exercises and went all out on them?

You could go to your local playground, find a set of monkey bars, and do as many pull-ups as you can without stopping. Then you could drop down and repeat the same process with push-ups: do as many as you can in one go. Once that’s done, you could walk to a nearby field, kick off your shoes, and start sprinting as fast as you can. Maybe you can’t run that fast or can only jog or do a fast walk. It doesn’t matter. Just move yourself across the field as fast as you can. Once you can’t take another step, the workout is over.

The benefits of a simple yet intense workout like this are far-reaching. When you’re done, your heart will be racing, your lungs will feel like they’re about to burst, and your face and body will be flushed and dripping with sweat. The muscles in your legs, arms, chest, and back will feel sore and heavy. You will feel like you actually accomplished something, and in fact you have: in the days to come, your heart, lungs, blood vessels, muscles, and even bones will begin making adaptations to high-intensity activity. To support these adaptations, your appetite will increase as will your drive for sleep. Repeat a few of these workouts and you’ll notice your physique start to transform: your waist will be slimmer and you’ll notice more definition in your arms and legs. Motivation will skyrocket.

Upping the Intensity Also Reduces Your Exercise Costs

Upping the intensity of your workouts not only brings a broader, more pronounced range of benefits, it also allows you to cut the costs of your workouts further. How? Well, when you work out with intensity, you won’t be able to continue for very long. Your workouts will become shorter by necessity, saving you time, and further reducing the psychological barrier standing between you and exercise.

Think about it: if your workouts typically involve long periods of steady-state cardio, how likely are you to squeeze that in after a long day at work or when you’re back-to-back with activities? On the other hand, if your “cardio” consists of one minute of all-out sprinting, you’re much more likely to fit that in, no matter what scheduling constraints you face.

Manage Your Motivation, Don’t Ignore It

The overall point here is that your motivation to work out is something that you can, and should be, managing throughout your training career.

The worst possible, though most repeated advice, is to “do it anyway” — to basically ignore your motivation or lack therefore and rely on willpower to get through your workout. Not only is this advice ineffectual, (as evidenced by the droves of people who never find the willpower to work out), it also rests on a fundamental misunderstanding of, and disrespect for, our emotional drives as animals.

The feeling of low motivation is meant to protect us from doing stupid, unnecessary shit. When it comes to exercise, it is a sign that your routine literally isn’t worth it. And in many cases, it absolutely isn’t. Is it really worth the 15 minute commute to the gym so you can pedal mindlessly on an exercise bike in front of a bank of television screens blaring CNN and Fox News? No, it isn’t. In which case, aren’t you better off finding another way to exercise your body?

Contrary to popular belief, your workout should excite you and enrich your mental resources. It should be a bright spot in your life, a highlight of your day. At the very least, it should be unobtrusive. Don’t settle for anything less.
